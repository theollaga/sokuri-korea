Learning even a few basic Korean phrases can dramatically improve your experience in Korea. While many Koreans in urban areas speak English, making an effort to communicate in Korean is appreciated and often leads to warmer interactions. This guide provides essential, practical phrases for everyday situations, including greetings, shopping, dining, directions, and emergencies. Each phrase includes the Korean script (Hangul), romanization, and English meaning.

## Why Learn Basic Korean Phrases?

- **Shows Respect**: Effort to speak the local language is seen as polite and respectful.
- **Better Service**: Staff often respond more warmly when greeted in Korean.
- **Easier Navigation**: Simple phrases help with directions, transportation, and shopping.
- **Emergency Preparedness**: Knowing how to ask for help can be crucial in urgent situations.
- **Cultural Connection**: Language is a gateway to deeper cultural understanding.

## Pronunciation Tips

- **Hangul is Phonetic**: Each letter corresponds to a sound; once you learn the letters, you can read any word.
- **Romanization Guide**: We use the Revised Romanization of Korean, the official system.
- **Speech Levels**: Korean has formal and informal speech. For foreigners, using polite formal speech (ending in -요 or -습니다) is safest in most situations.
- **Listening Practice**: Try to mimic the rhythm and intonation; Korean is syllable-timed.

## Greetings and Essentials

These are the most fundamental phrases you’ll use daily.

| Korean (Hangul) | Romanization | English | Usage Note |
|-----------------|--------------|---------|------------|
| 안녕하세요 | annyeonghaseyo | Hello / Good day | Standard polite greeting for any time of day. |
| 안녕 | annyeong | Hi / Bye | Informal; use only with close friends or children. |
| 감사합니다 | gamsahamnida | Thank you | Polite thanks; high frequency of use. |
| 고마워요 | gomawoyo | Thanks | Slightly less formal but still polite. |
| 죄송합니다 | joesonghamnida | I’m sorry / Excuse me | For apologizing or getting attention. |
| 실례합니다 | silyehamnida | Excuse me | To get someone’s attention politely. |
| 네 | ne | Yes | Polite affirmation. |
| 아니요 | aniyo | No | Polite negation. |
| 괜찮아요 | gwaenchanayo | It’s okay / I’m fine | Use to reassure or decline politely. |
| 도와주세요 | dowajuseyo | Please help me | Essential for emergencies. |
| 화장실 어디에요? | hwajangsil eodieyo? | Where is the bathroom? | Very useful when out and about. |

## Shopping and Services

Whether you’re at a market, department store, or convenience store, these phrases will help.

| Korean (Hangul) | Romanization | English | Usage Note |
|-----------------|--------------|---------|------------|
| 얼마예요? | eolmayeyo? | How much is it? | Point to the item while asking. |
| 깎아 주세요 | kkakka juseyo | Can you lower the price? | Bargaining is common in markets but not in fixed-price stores. |
| 이거 주세요 | igeo juseyo | I’ll take this one. | Point or show the item. |
| 다른 게 있어요? | dareun ge isseoyo? | Do you have something else? | Ask for alternatives. |
| 영수증 주세요 | yeongsujeung juseyo | Can I have a receipt? | Useful for expense tracking. |
| 카드 돼요? | keudeu dwaeyo? | Does it take card? | Check if credit/debit card is accepted. |
| 현금만 돼요? | hyeongeumman dwaeyo? | Cash only? | Some small vendors prefer cash. |
| 봉투 주세요 | bongtu juseyo | Can I have a bag? | Many stores charge for bags; specify if you need one. |
| 이거 맞아요? | igeo majayo? | Is this correct? | Use when checking change or an order. |

### At a Convenience Store (GS25, CU, 7-Eleven)

- **직원**: 안녕하세요! (Employee: Hello!)
- **Customer**: 이거 주세요. (I’ll take this.)
- **직원**: 봉투 필요하세요? (Do you need a bag?)
- **Customer**: 네, 주세요. (Yes, please.)
- **직원**: 얼마예요? (How much is it?)
- **Customer**: Here you hand over T-money or card.
- **직원**: 감사합니다. (Thank you.)

## Dining and Restaurants

Food is a highlight of any trip to Korea. These phrases will help you navigate menus and interact with staff.

| Korean (Hangul) | Romanization | English | Usage Note |
|-----------------|--------------|---------|------------|
| 메뉴 주세요 | menyu juseyo | Can I have the menu? | Ask when you’re seated. |
| 추천해 주세요 | chucheonhae juseyo | Can you recommend something? | Great for discovering local specialties. |
| 이거 뭐예요? | igeo mwoyeyo? | What is this? | Point to a menu item or dish. |
| 매운 걸 못 먹어요 | maeun geol mok meogoyo | I can’t eat spicy food. | Important for those with low spice tolerance. |
| 덜 맵게 해주세요 | deol maebge haejuseyo | Please make it less spicy. | Common request at many restaurants. |
| 물 주세요 | mul juseyo | Can I have some water? | Water is often free; you can ask for a refill. |
| 반찬 더 주세요 | banchan deo juseyo | Can I have more banchan? | Side dishes are often refillable. |
| 계산해 주세요 | gyesanhae juseyo | Can we have the bill? | Ask when you’re ready to pay. |
| 따로 주세요 | ttaro juseyo | Please pack it to go. | For leftovers; many restaurants will comply. |
| 맛있어요! | masisseoyo! | It’s delicious! | Compliment the chef; always appreciated. |

### Example Dialogue at a Barbecue Restaurant

- **Staff**: 여기 앉으세요. (Please sit here.)
- **Customer**: 메뉴 주세요. (Can I have the menu?)
- **Staff**: 여기 있습니다. (Here it is.)
- **Customer**: 삼겹살 두 인분 주세요. (Two portions of samgyeopsal, please.)
- **Staff**: 알겠습니다. (Understood.)
- **After grilling...**
- **Customer**: 맛있어요! (It’s delicious!)
- **Staff**: 감사합니다. (Thank you.)
- **Customer**: 계산해 주세요. (Can we have the bill?)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need to be fluent in Korean to get by in Korea?**  
A: No, many people in Seoul, Busan, and other major cities speak English, especially in tourist areas, hotels, and large companies. However, knowing basic phrases will greatly improve your daily interactions and is appreciated by locals.

**Q: Is it rude to speak English in Korea?**  
A: Not at all. Koreans understand that English is a global language and many study it in school. Speaking English is perfectly acceptable; attempting Korean is seen as a bonus.

**Q: Should I use formal or informal speech?**  
A: As a foreigner, it’s safest to use polite formal speech (ending in -요 or -습니다) in almost all situations. Reserve informal speech (반말) for close friends or when someone explicitly tells you it’s okay to speak casually.

**Q: How do I say “I don’t understand”?**  
A: 한국말 잘 못해요 (hangukmal jal mothaeyo) – “I don’t speak Korean well.” or 다시 한 번 말해 주세요 (dashi han beon malhae juseyo) – “Please say it again.”

**Q: Are there differences between regional dialects?**  
A: Yes, there are regional accents and vocabularies (e.g., Busan dialect, Jeju dialect), but standard Korean based on Seoul is understood everywhere. Don’t worry about dialect differences as a beginner.

**Q: How do I read Korean menus if I don’t know Hangul?**  
A: Many restaurants in tourist areas have English menus or pictures. You can also use translation apps like Papago or Google Translate to scan and translate text in real time.

## Conclusion

Equipping yourself with these essential Korean phrases will open doors to more meaningful interactions, smoother daily life, and a deeper appreciation of Korean culture. Remember, perfection is not expected—locals appreciate the effort and warmth behind your attempt to speak their language. Start with a few phrases each day, practice them in real situations, and soon you’ll find yourself navigating Korea with greater confidence and connection.
